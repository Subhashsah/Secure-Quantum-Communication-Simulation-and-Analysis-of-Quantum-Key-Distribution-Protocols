1. Introduction
The BBM92 protocol is an entanglement-based Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) protocol proposed in 1992 by Charles H. Bennett, Gilles Brassard, and N. David Mermin.

BBM92 can be understood as the entanglement-based counterpart of BB84.
While BB84 relies on a sender preparing and transmitting quantum states, BBM92 relies on pairs of entangled particles distributed to the communicating parties.

2. Core Idea of BBM92
Comparison with BB84

BB84:
Sender prepares single photons
Security arises from measurement disturbance

BBM92:
A source prepares entangled photon pairs
One photon is sent to Alice, the other to Bob
Security arises from entanglement correlations

The key insight of BBM92 is that neither Alice nor Bob needs to trust the source, as security is verified through correlation statistics.

3. Quantum States and Measurement Bases
3.1 Entangled State
The source typically generates a maximally entangled Bell state, such as:

∣Φ+⟩=(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)/√2

This state ensures strong correlations when measured in the same basis.

3.2 Measurement Bases
Both Alice and Bob randomly choose between two mutually unbiased bases:
Z basis (rectilinear / computational):
∣0⟩,∣1⟩

X basis (diagonal):
∣+⟩,∣−⟩

These are the same bases used in BB84, ensuring conceptual continuity.

4. Working Principle of the BBM92 Protocol

Step 1: Entangled Photon Generation
A quantum source produces pairs of entangled photons
    One photon is sent to Alice
    The other photon is sent to Bob
    At this stage, no classical bits exist.

Step 2: Random Basis Selection

    Alice randomly selects either the Z or X basis
    Bob independently selects either the Z or X basis
    This randomness is essential for security.

Step 3: Quantum Measurement
Alice measures her photon in the chosen basis
Bob measures his photon in the chosen basis

Due to entanglement:
If bases match → results are strongly correlated
If bases differ → outcomes are random

Step 4: Basis Reconciliation (Classical Channel)
Alice and Bob publicly announce only the measurement bases
Measurement outcomes remain secret
Rounds where bases do not match are discarded

Step 5: Raw Key Generation

Remaining correlated measurement outcomes form the raw key
These bits are identical in the absence of noise and attacks

5. Eavesdropping Detection Using QBER
5.1 Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER)

The Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) is computed by publicly comparing a randomly selected subset of the raw key:

QBER=Total compared bits/Number of mismatched bits
	​

5.2 Security Criterion

QBER ≈ 0:
Strong entanglement correlations

Channel considered secure
QBER > threshold:

Correlations degraded
Presence of noise or eavesdropping suspected
Protocol is aborted

An eavesdropper attempting to intercept or measure the entangled photons inevitably disturbs the correlations, increasing QBER.

6. Error Correction and Privacy Amplification
If QBER is below the acceptable threshold:
    Error correction is applied to reconcile residual mismatches
    Privacy amplification is performed to reduce any partial information potentially gained by an eavesdropper
    A final secure secret key is obtained

7. Security Characteristics of BBM92
Security relies on quantum entanglement
Measurement disturbance reveals eavesdropping
Equivalent security to BB84 under ideal conditions
Conceptually simpler than E91 (no Bell-test required)

BBM92 can be viewed as a bridge between BB84 and E91:
Like BB84 → uses basis matching and QBER
Like E91 → uses entangled states

8. BBM92 vs BB84 vs E91 (Conceptual Positioning)
| Protocol | Photon Source      | Security Test              |
| -------- | ------------------ | -------------------------- |
| BB84     | Prepared by sender | QBER                       |
| BBM92    | Entangled source   | QBER                       |
| E91      | Entangled source   | Bell–CHSH violation + QBER |

9. Advantages and Limitations
Advantages
Eliminates need for trusted state preparation
Naturally suited for entanglement-based systems
Strong conceptual foundation

Limitations
Requires reliable entangled photon sources
Does not directly certify non-locality (unlike E91)

10. Conclusion
The BBM92 protocol extends the principles of BB84 into an entanglement-based framework, combining the simplicity of basis reconciliation with the physical security advantages of quantum entanglement. By relying on QBER to detect disturbances in correlated measurements, BBM92 enables secure key generation while reducing assumptions about the sender’s state preparation. This makes BBM92 a conceptually important and practically relevant protocol in quantum cryptography.
