BB84 Quantum Key Distribution Protocol
1. Introduction to BB84
The BB84 protocol, proposed by Charles Bennett and Gilles Brassard in 1984, is the first and most widely studied Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) protocol.It enables two legitimate parties—commonly referred to as Subhash (sender) and David (receiver)—to establish a shared secret key using the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics.

The security of BB84 is guaranteed by:
 - The no-cloning theorem
 - The disturbance caused by quantum measurement
 - The randomness of quantum basis selection
Any attempt by an unauthorized entity to intercept the communication introduces detectable errors in the generated key.

2. Quantum Bases Used in BB84
BB84 employs two mutually unbiased polarization bases to encode classical information into quantum states.

2.1 Z-Basis (Rectilinear / Computational Basis)
In the Z-basis, bits are encoded as:

Bit 0 → ∣0⟩ → Horizontal polarization (0°)

Bit 1 → ∣1⟩ → Vertical polarization (90°)

This basis corresponds to the standard computational basis:
        {∣0⟩,∣1⟩}

2.2 X-Basis (Diagonal Basis)
In the X-basis, bits are encoded using superposition states:

Bit 0 → ∣+⟩ → +45°
Bit 1 → ∣−⟩ → −45°

These states represent superposition, a key quantum property that ensures security.

3. Working Principle of the BB84 Protocol
The BB84 protocol proceeds through the following steps:
Step 1: Random Bit Generation (Subhash)
Subhash generates a random classical bit-string:
1 0 0 1 1 0 1 1

Step 2: Random Basis Selection (Subhash)
For each bit, Subhash randomly selects either:
Z-basis (+)
X-basis (×)
This randomness is fundamental to the protocol’s security.

Step 3: Quantum State Encoding and Transmission
Based on the chosen basis:
Z-basis: 0 → |0⟩, 1 → |1⟩
X-basis: 0 → |+⟩, 1 → |−⟩
Each encoded qubit is transmitted to David as a polarized photon through the quantum channel.

Step 4: Measurement by David
David independently and randomly selects a measurement basis (Z or X) for each received photon.
If David’s basis matches Subhash’s basis → correct bit is obtained
If the bases differ → the measurement outcome is random

Step 5: Basis Reconciliation and Key Sifting
Subhash and David publicly compare their chosen bases over a classical channel.
Bits corresponding to matching bases are retained
All other bits are discarded
The remaining sequence forms the shifted (sifted) key.

5. Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER)
5.1 Definition
The Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) is a fundamental metric used to evaluate the security and reliability of a QKD system. It represents the fraction of bits in the sifted key where Subhash’s and David’s bit values do not match.

QBER acts as a security indicator, revealing noise or the presence of an eavesdropper.

5.2 Mathematical Expression
QBER=𝑁error/𝑁total
Where:

𝑁error: Number of mismatched bits
𝑁total: Total number of compared sifted bits

QBER is expressed as a percentage.

5.3 QBER Calculation Example
If:

Total sifted bits = 100
Mismatched bits = 7

QBER = 7/100 *100%

5.4 Sources of QBER
QBER may arise due to:
Quantum channel noise
Detector imperfections
Polarization misalignment
Eavesdropping attempts

Among these, eavesdropping causes a significant and statistically observable increase in QBER.

5.5 QBER Threshold in BB84
For the BB84 protocol:

QBER ≤ 11% → Secure key generation possible
QBER > 11% → Key is discarded and communication is aborted

This threshold ensures unconditional security.

6. Bloch Sphere Representation of Qubit States
The Bloch sphere provides a geometric visualization of qubit states:

|0⟩ → Vector pointing to the north pole (+Z axis)
|1⟩ → Vector pointing to the south pole (−Z axis)
|+⟩ → Vector on the equator along +X axis
|−⟩ → Vector on the equator along −X axis

These representations clearly illustrate the difference between computational and diagonal basis states and help visualize quantum superposition and measurement behavior
