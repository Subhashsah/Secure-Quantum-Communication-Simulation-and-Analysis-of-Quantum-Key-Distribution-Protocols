Note:
Alice and Bob decide before the protocol starts which basis pairs are for key generation and which are for Bell testing.
After measurement, they only classify each row according to that pre-decided rule, once the bases are publicly announced.

Step 0: Pre-agreement (before any photons are sent)
Alice and Bob agree on two things:

(a) Allowed bases
Alice: A1, A2, A3
Bob: B1, B2, B3

(b) Classification rule (VERY IMPORTANT)
For example:

Key bases → (A1,B1), (A2,B2)

Bell-test bases → (A1,B3), (A2,B1), (A2,B3), (A3,B1), …


Step 1: Entangled photons are generated
Source sends one photon to Alice, one to Bob
No bits exist yet

Step 2: Measurement
Alice and Bob randomly choose bases
They measure and record:
 - basis used
 - result obtained

At this moment:
 - They do not know whether a row is key or Bell
 - They do not communicate

Step 3: Public announcement of bases (NOT results)
Over a public channel, they announce only:
 - “I used A2 on pair 5”
 - “I used B1 on pair 5”

Still:
Results remain secret
No decision yet per row

Step 4: Row classification (using pre-decided rule)
Now they look at each row:

If basis pair ∈ key list → 🔑 key row
If basis pair ∈ Bell list → 🔒 security row
Otherwise → ❌ discard

⚠️ This is classification, not decision-making.

Before any photons are sent, Alice and Bob communicate over a classical channel and pre-agree on:
 - Which measurement bases each of them may use
 - Which pairs of bases are reserved for key generation
 - Which pairs of bases are reserved for Bell-inequality (security) testing

 Classical agreement first → Quantum transmission → Random measurement → Public basis announcement → Fixed classification

 The B92 protocol is a quantum key distribution (QKD) protocol proposed in 1992 by Charles H. Bennett.
 It is a simplified version of BB84, using only two non-orthogonal quantum states instead of four.
 “Two quantum states are non-orthogonal if their inner product is non-zero, meaning they cannot be perfectly distinguished by any measurement. In B92, non-orthogonal states provide security against eavesdropping.”
 
In quantum mechanics, two states are orthogonal if they can be perfectly distinguished by a measurement.

Orthogonal states → inner product = 0

Non-orthogonal states → inner product ≠ 0
